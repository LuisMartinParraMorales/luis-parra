# Portfolio Writing Voice

## Core Voice

Write in the first person.

The tone should feel:
- warm
- technically serious
- clear
- confident without inflation
- curious and generous in explanation

The site should project Luis as:
- a strong engineer
- a thoughtful builder
- someone who enjoys explaining technical work clearly
- someone who can work across technical and non-technical contexts
- someone who cares about delivery, maintainability, and trust

## Writing Principles

- Prefer `I built`, `I designed`, `I worked on`, `I learned`, and `I chose` over passive phrasing.
- Use present tense for current working style and engineering philosophy.
- Use past tense for specific project decisions, delivered systems, and shipped work.
- Prefer mechanism over adjectives.
- Prefer evidence over self-description.
- Prefer clear system language over corporate or startup hype.
- Let the writing sound human, not polished into blandness.

## What To Signal

The writing should make it obvious that Luis is:
- backend-heavy
- strong in analytical and algorithmic systems
- serious about product delivery, not just prototypes
- collaborative and able to communicate with real users and stakeholders
- passionate about technical work, but grounded in outcomes and trust

Show these traits through examples, decisions, and tradeoffs instead of repeatedly naming them.

## Good Patterns

Stronger:

`I like turning messy analytical workflows into products that people can actually use, inspect, and trust.`

`I start from the scoring logic, routing workflow, or simulation output, then shape the interface around that core.`

`I use AI to accelerate engineering work, but I still optimize for typed contracts, maintainable services, and reliable deployment.`

Weaker:

`I am a passionate engineer who loves solving hard problems.`

`I am results-driven and committed to innovation.`

`I leverage cutting-edge AI and modern technologies to create impactful solutions.`

## Tone To Avoid

Avoid:
- corporate CV filler
- generic “problem solver” language
- stack-dump writing
- exaggerated claims
- “thought leader” phrasing
- robotic AI-polished copy

Avoid phrases like:
- `passionate problem solver`
- `results-driven professional`
- `leveraged cutting-edge AI`
- `full-spectrum innovation`

## Project Story Formula

For project case studies, default to this structure:

1. Here was the problem.
2. Here is what I built.
3. Here is what made it hard.
4. Here is the decision or tradeoff that mattered.
5. Here is why the system matters.

## Final Rule

Do not repeatedly tell the reader that Luis is passionate, collaborative, curious, or rigorous.

Write in a way that makes the reader feel those qualities through the clarity of the explanation, the precision of the technical detail, and the honesty of the tradeoffs.
